What Is a Certified Translation and When Do You Need One?

✦ Ricardo Eva Magdaleno 📅 May 2025 ⏱ 5 min read
← Back to Blog

If you've ever applied for a visa, submitted documents to a foreign university, or dealt with legal paperwork across borders, you've likely encountered the requirement for a certified translation. But what exactly does that mean — and how is it different from a regular translation?

In this article, we'll break down exactly what certified translation is, when it's required, and why working with a qualified professional matters more than you might think.

What Makes a Translation "Certified"?

A certified translation is a translated document accompanied by a signed statement — typically from the translator or translation agency — attesting that the translation is a complete and accurate representation of the original document. This statement is what gives the translation its official, legal weight.

In Panama and many other countries, the translator must also be officially recognized by the relevant authority. In Panama, certified translators are registered with the Ministerio de Educación (MEDUCA), which grants the legal standing to produce certified translations accepted by courts, government agencies, and international institutions.

✦ Important

Not all translations are certified translations. A regular translation — even a high-quality one — does not carry official legal validity unless accompanied by the required certification statement and credentials.

When Is a Certified Translation Required?

You'll need a certified translation in a wide range of situations, including:

  • Immigration and visa applications — birth certificates, marriage certificates, criminal records, and other personal documents submitted to embassies or immigration offices
  • Academic admissions — transcripts, diplomas, and degrees submitted to foreign universities or educational institutions
  • Legal proceedings — contracts, court orders, powers of attorney, and affidavits used in Panamanian or international courts
  • Business and corporate filings — articles of incorporation, financial statements, shareholder agreements for cross-border operations
  • Medical records — patient histories and clinical documents required by foreign healthcare providers or insurance companies
  • Real estate transactions — property deeds and title documents used internationally
"A certified translation is not just a language service — it is a legal document. Its accuracy and authenticity can directly impact the outcome of immigration cases, court proceedings, and international business deals."

Certified vs. Notarized vs. Apostilled: What's the Difference?

These three terms are often confused, but they refer to different levels of document authentication:

Certified Translation

The translator signs and stamps a statement confirming the accuracy of the translation. This is the baseline requirement for most official submissions.

Notarized Translation

The translator's signature is verified by a notary public. Some institutions — particularly in the United States — require this additional layer of authentication. The notary is not verifying the quality of the translation itself, only the identity of the signer.

Apostilled Translation

An apostille is an internationally recognized certificate that authenticates the origin of a public document for use in countries that are signatories to the Hague Convention. Panama is a signatory, so documents destined for use abroad often require both a certified translation and an apostille from the Panamanian Ministry of Foreign Affairs.

✦ Pro Tip

Always confirm with the receiving institution exactly what level of authentication they require before ordering your translation. Requirements vary significantly by country and institution type.

Why It Matters Who Does the Translation

A certified translation is only as valuable as the credentials behind it. Using an unqualified translator — even one who is bilingual — can result in documents being rejected, delays in legal proceedings, or worse, legal complications from inaccurate translations of technical or legal terminology.
