How to Translate Documents for Official Use in Panama

✦ Ricardo Eva Magdaleno📅 March 2025⏱ 6 min read
← Back to Blog

Panama's position as a global hub for commerce, immigration, and international business means that official documents frequently cross borders — and with them comes a maze of requirements: apostilles, notarizations, sworn translations, and more. If you've ever tried to navigate this process alone, you know how confusing it can be.

This guide will walk you through exactly what's required to have documents officially translated for use in Panama, or to have Panamanian documents accepted abroad.

Understanding the Three Levels of Document Authentication

1. Certified Translation

The foundation of any official translation. A certified translator registered with Panama's Ministerio de Educación (MEDUCA) produces the translation and signs a statement confirming its accuracy. This is required for virtually all official submissions.

2. Notarization

Some institutions — particularly in the United States and Canada — require that the translator's signature be notarized by a public notary. The notary does not verify the translation's accuracy; they verify the identity and signature of the translator.

3. Apostille

An apostille is an international certification issued under the Hague Convention that authenticates the origin of a public document for use in foreign countries. Panama is a signatory to the Hague Convention. Apostilles for Panamanian documents are issued by the Ministerio de Relaciones Exteriores (MRE).

✦ Important Note

An apostille authenticates the document itself — not the translation. If a translated document requires an apostille, both the original and the translation may each need separate apostilles, depending on the receiving country's requirements.

Common Scenarios and What You'll Need

Foreign documents being used in Panama

  • Certified translation into Spanish by a MEDUCA-registered translator
  • Apostille on the original document (if issued in a Hague Convention country)
  • Notarization may be required by specific institutions

Panamanian documents being used abroad

  • Certified translation into the destination country's language
  • Apostille from Panama's MRE on the original Panamanian document
  • Some countries require additional embassy legalization (consular legalization) if not a Hague signatory

Step-by-Step: Getting Your Documents Officially Translated

  1. Identify your requirement. Contact the receiving institution (embassy, university, court, government agency) to confirm exactly what they require: certified translation only, notarization, apostille, or a combination.
  2. Gather your original documents. Provide clear, legible copies — scanned PDFs are ideal. For apostille purposes, originals are typically required.
  3. Submit to a MEDUCA-registered translator. Your translator will produce the certified translation with official seal and signature.
  4. Obtain apostille if required. Submit your original document to Panama's MRE for apostille processing, or to the relevant authority in the document's country of origin.
  5. Submit to receiving institution. Deliver your complete document package — original, apostille, and certified translation — to the requesting authority.
"Knowing exactly what each institution requires before you start will save you significant time and money. Requirements vary widely — and the cost of an incorrect submission can be a rejected visa or a delayed legal proceeding."
